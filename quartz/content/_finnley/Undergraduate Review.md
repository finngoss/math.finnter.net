As listed as preliminary in the math grad handbook.
# A. Linear Algebra

Essential Requirements
1. Gaussian elimination ("row reduction") and applications to systems of equations, ranks.
2. Vector spaces and linear transformations, subspaces, basis, replacement theorem. Kernel and
image of a linear transformation.
3. Basis of a vector space. The matrix of a linear transformation with respect to two bases. Change
of basis relation to similar matrices.
4. Determinants, traces, characteristic polynomial.
5. Eigenvectors and eigenvalues. Canonical forms including Jordan form. Minimal polynomial. Two
criterion of diagonalizability (1) dimension of eigenspaces = algebraic multiplicity (2) minimal
polynomial has no repeated roots.
6. Euclidean spaces. Orthonormal bases and the Gram-Schmidt process, relation with QR
decomposition. Orthogonal complement, orthogonal projection.
7. Self-adjoint operators and symmetric matrices. Spectral theorems, Singular Value
Decomposition.

More advanced topics
1. Row rank equals column rank and relation to maximal invertible square submatrix
2. Direct sums, quotient spaces, fundamental theorem of linear operators: the image of a linear map
f is isomorphic to the quotient of the domain of f by the kernel of f. The dual space and the
transpose of a linear transformation.
3. Equivalence of the following (1) finding a basis such that the matrix representing a linear
transform is in a simple form (2) canonical form of a matrix.
4. Vandermonde determinant. The determinant, trace, and characteristic polynomial in terms of the
eigenvalues. Cayley-Hamilton Theorem.
5. Decompose space into direct sum of generalized eigenspaces. Nilpotent operator. Simultaneous
diagonalization.
6. Hermitian spaces, Orthonormal Basis, decomposition of any matrix as a product of Hermitian and
unitary. Bilinear forms, canonical form of quadratic and skew-symmetric bilinear forms,
signatures.
7. Positive definite symmetric matrices. Normal operators, unitary operators. Rayleigh quotient.

# B. Vector Calculus and Differential Equations
Essential requirements
1. The derivative of a function 𝑓𝑓: 𝑅𝑅𝑛𝑛 → 𝑅𝑅𝑛𝑛. Linear approximation.
2. Line integrals. The multidimensional Riemann integral. Change of variables formula.
3. Div, grad, curl and relations between them. Divergence theorem, Stokes' theorem, Green's
theorem.
4. Existence and uniqueness of solutions to a first order Ordinary Differential Equations (ODE).
5. First and second order linear ODEs with constant coefficients.
6. Higher order linear ODEs and systems of first order ODEs.

More advanced topics
7. Implicit and inverse function theorem
8. Multivariate Taylor series
9. Existence and Uniqueness theorem of systems of ODE

# C. Complex Analysis
Essential requirements
1. Holomorphic (analytic) functions.
2. Elementary functions and the mappings that they define.
3. Conformal mappings. Problems such as finding a conformal mapping from a quadrant to a disc.
4. The Taylor series of a holomorphic function.
5. Contour integrals. Cauchy's theorem. The Cauchy formula.
6. Poles. Meromorphic functions. Laurent series. Residues.
7. Evaluating contour integrals of meromorphic functions.
8. Using complex integration for evaluating some improper integrals.

# D. Abstract Algebra
Essential requirements
1. Basic examples of groups: cyclic groups, dihedral groups, permutation groups, alternating groups, and matrix groups.
2. Subgroups, homomorphisms, and isomorphisms.
3. Cosets, quotients, Lagrange’s theorem, and the first isomorphism theorem.
4. Rings, ideals, and quotient rings.
5. Integral domains, prime ideals and maximal ideals.
6. Principal ideals and principal ideal domains (PIDs). Examples such as Z and F[x].
7. Unique factorization in PIDs.
8. Finite and algebraic extensions, splitting fields, and finite fields.

More advanced topics
9. Structure of finitely generated abelian groups.
10. Group actions on sets. Orbits–stabilizers theorem and applications.
11. Simple groups and the Sylow theorems.
12. Geometric constructions of fields and Galois Theory
13. Galois theory and solvability by radicals.

# E. Real Analysis
Essential Requirements
1. Limits and continuity, including application of the 𝜀𝜀 − 𝛿𝛿 definition.
2. Integrals, including improper integrals. Convergence tests.
3. Sequences and series. Taylor series. Radius of convergence.
4. Series of functions. Uniform convergence.
5. Exchanging the order of summation and limit.
6. Differentiation and integration of series of functions.
7. Countable and uncountable sets. Axiom of choice and Zorn's lemma.
8. Axioms for R.

More advanced topics
9. Lebesgue integral

# F. Point Set Topology
1. Basics of set theory, inclusion/subset, injection, surjection, bijection.
2. Open, closed, connected, and compact subsets of $\R^n$. Heine-Borel theorem.
3. Topological spaces and Hausdorff spaces.
4. Metric spaces as topological spaces.
5. Continuous functions.
6. Compact sets. Images of compact sets are compact. Continuous functions achieve their maximum on compact sets.
7. Connected sets. Images of connected sets are connected.

Advanced topics:
8. Tychonoff Theorem,
9. Metricization
10. Fundamental group.
